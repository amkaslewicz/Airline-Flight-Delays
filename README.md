# U.S. Flight Delay Analysis

## 1. Purpose of the Analysis

This project analyzes U.S. airline delay data from the Bureau of Transportation Statistics (BTS) to examine patterns in flight delays across airlines, airports, time, delay causes, and external conditions. The analysis focuses on identifying trends in both delay frequency and severity, with particular attention to the role of weather.

The project combines system-wide exploratory data analysis with a focused case study of New York City airports (JFK, LGA, EWR) to assess how weather-related delays have evolved from 2005 to 2025. The goal is to identify broader system patterns and determine whether weather-related disruptions are becoming more frequent, more severe, or changing in nature over time.

## 2. Data Sources and How to Obtain Them
All data files are located in the folder "data"

### Airline Delay Data

Source: U.S. Bureau of Transportation Statistics (BTS)  
Dataset: Airline On-Time Performance Data  

- Website: https://www.transtats.bts.gov/

Data includes:
- Total flights 
- Delayed flights (15+ minutes)
- Delay causes (carrier, weather, NAS, security, late aircraft)
- Total delay minutes

To reproduce this project:

1. Navigate to: https://www.transtats.bts.gov/
2. Select “Aviation” → “Airline On-Time Performance Data” → "Reporting Carrier On-Time Performance (1987-present)" → "download"
3. Select your desired filters:
   - Time period (e.g., 2005–2025 for case study, 2021–2025 for system analysis)
   - Airports or states (optional; e.g., New York for NYC case study)
4. Download the data as CSV files


### Airport Location Data
Source: OurAirports  
Dataset: airports.csv

- Website: https://ourairports.com/data/

Data includes:
- Airport identifiers (IATA/ICAO codes)
- Latitude and longitude
- Airport names and locations

This data is used to:
- Merge with airline delay data using airport codes
- Create spatial visualizations of airport-level delay patterns

To reproduce this project:

1. Navigate to: https://ourairports.com/data/
2. Download:
   - `airports.csv`

## 3. Scripts and Execution Order
All scripts are located in the folder "scripts"

They should be run in the following order:

1. 01_eda.py

This script performs the main system-wide exploratory data analysis using the airline delay dataset. It generates summary statistics and visualizations showing:

overall delay patterns over time
distributions of delay rates and delay minutes
airline-level comparisons
airport-level comparisons
seasonal patterns
delay cause composition

This script provides the broad descriptive foundation for the project.

2. 02_maps.py

This script prepares airport-level and seasonal spatial data for mapping. It:

loads the airline delay dataset
merges airport delay metrics with latitude and longitude data from OurAirports
creates map-ready files for airport-level and seasonal analysis
exports these files for use in QGIS or other mapping tools

This script supports the geographic component of the project.

3. 03_predictive_model.py

This script develops predictive models to estimate:

delay probability
delay severity
the most likely cause of delay

It uses airport, carrier, and month as predictors and outputs model performance metrics and example predictions. This script serves as an extension to test how predictable flight delays are using the available data.

4. 04_nyc_case_study.py

This script conducts the focused case study of New York City airports (JFK, LGA, and EWR). It:

combines the three airport datasets
constructs adjusted weather-related delay measures
analyzes long-term trends in weather delay probability and severity
examines the frequency of severe weather-delay months
calculates weather’s share of total delay minutes

This script contains the main substantive findings of the project on how weather-related disruption has changed over time.

To run the full project from start to finish:

python scripts/01_eda.py
python scripts/02_maps.py
python scripts/03_predictive_model.py
python scripts/04_nyc_case_study.py

All outputs will be saved automatically in the outputs folder.

## 5. Results

### System-Wide Patterns

The exploratory analysis shows that flight delays are a persistent feature of the U.S. aviation system, with approximately 20% of flights experiencing delays of 15 minutes or more. When delays occur, they are substantial, averaging around 70 minutes among delayed flights. 

Trends over time indicate that delays do not increase steadily, but instead fluctuate with periods of concentrated disruption. This suggests that delays are driven by episodic system stress rather than gradual deterioration.


### Airline and Airport Differences

There is significant variation in delay performance across airlines and airports. Some airlines exhibit lower delay frequency but higher delay severity, indicating fewer but more disruptive delays. At the airport level, differences suggest that congestion, infrastructure, and traffic volume play an important role in shaping delay outcomes.


### Seasonality

Delays follow a clear seasonal pattern, with peaks during the summer months (June–July) and lower levels in early fall. This pattern aligns with periods of higher travel demand, suggesting that system congestion is a key driver of delays. Seasonal variation is also evident in delay severity, with more severe disruptions occurring during peak travel periods.


### Role of Weather (NYC Case Study)

The case study of New York City airports (JFK, LGA, and EWR) provides a more detailed view of weather-related delays.

Results show that:
- Weather accounts for approximately 20–30% of total delay minutes
- The probability of weather-related delays has remained relatively stable or declined slightly over time
- The severity of weather-related delays has increased, particularly in recent years
- Severe weather events occur more frequently in later years compared to earlier periods

These findings indicate that while weather is not the primary source of delays, it plays an important role in driving high-impact disruptions.


### Weather as a System Amplifier

Across analyses, weather appears to act less as a direct cause of delays and more as a stressor that amplifies existing system constraints. During periods of high demand or congestion, weather events lead to disproportionately large disruptions, suggesting that the system is vulnerable to external shocks.


### Predictive Modeling

The predictive models developed in this project show limited ability to explain or forecast delays:

- Delay probability R²: 0.1078  
- Delay severity R²: 0.0577  
- Cause classification accuracy: 0.5565  

These results indicate that:
- A large portion of delay variation is unexplained by observable variables
- Delay severity is highly unpredictable
- Delay causes are difficult to classify accurately


### Overall Conclusion

The results suggest that flight delays are not driven by a single factor such as weather, but instead emerge from a complex system involving airline operations, airport congestion, and air traffic control constraints.

Weather contributes to delays, but primarily acts as an amplifier of existing inefficiencies rather than the primary cause. 

Overall, flight delays should be understood as a system-level phenomenon rather than a problem attributable to any single cause.
